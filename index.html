How did this happen??

February 2011

A a bored George, about to move to Southern Georgia, decides to do a global search for "best match" on a popular dating site. On the first page of results is a woman with almost the same username (a reference to the well-known computer scientist Edsger Dijkstra). He decides to send a message:

Feb 2, 2011 - 6:44pm
I just wanted to apologize for all the quick-and-dirty hacks I've ever committed. (I didn't know any better; I'm only an engineer.)

Meanwhile...

One of Elaine's best friends, tired of the single life, has decided to sign up for a popular online dating site, but not alone—Elaine finds herself convinced to make an account, "just to keep me company". Less than two weeks later, Elaine receives a message from a mysterious engineer with nearly the same username as her. Sensing someone who might be up for some banter (and, ugh, he's cute too), she replies:

Feb 2, 2011 - 8:02pm
Oh, I know about you engineers -- I'm related to one and he writes the most painful-to-read code ever (why are ALL your variables global???) [Ed.: Sorry, Dale!] . On the bright side, engineers are much less likely to get caught up in "I will implement this in the most general way possible" and end up only finishing half of what they set out to do (not that I have ever done such a thing, of course).

A few weeks (and one nail-bitingly long gap in George's messages, thanks to that move) later, we know:

  1. Elaine and George both enjoy pretentious references, snappy wit, grandiose vocabulary, and all things computer-related.
  2. Despite living 2,500 miles apart on opposite sides of the country, their hometowns are less than a five-hour's drive apart in Pennsylvania and Upstate New York.
  3. Elaine is going to be in Georgia for the summer for a research internship and George owes her a drink (in exchange for the rights to use one of her cleverer lines).
  4. ... but George is going to be underway for the entire summer; there's no way they can meet up while Elaine is in Georgia.
  5. They can't quite bring themselves to let this chance slip away.

April 2011

Fortunately for everyone involved, Elaine has a bit of a taste for adventure [Ed.: Sorry, Mom!], and suggests meeting up in Austin, Texas, almost exactly halfway between Los Angeles and Saint Marys. Rooms are reserved at a grad-student-budget-friendly motel on the outskirts of downtown Austin, plane tickets are purchased, and April 1st, 2011 (yes, April Fool's Day) finds Elaine sitting outside a gate in the Delta terminal of the Austin airport, frantically drawing robots in a futile attempt to calm her nerves.

George steps off the plane looking even more engineer-ish than usual, but Elaine is charmed anyway. They hold hands in the taxi to the hotel, and spend the next day walking around Austin, still hand-in-hand. Less than a week after their trip, they decide to give a relationship a try.

March 2015

After four years, sixty thousand frequent flier miles, five 3-month stretches underway, more than 400 emails, endless video calls and chatting, one cross-country move, a shared lease, and two cats, they decide it's time to get married.

Hooray! How do I get there?

By Car

For most guests, the easiest way to get to State College will be by car. State College is smack dab in the middle of Pennsylvania which puts it within about a four- to five-hour drive from NYC, Shortsville, or DC.

By Plane

There are direct flights to State College from Philadelphia (US Airways), Washington Dulles and Chicago O'Hare (United), and Detroit (Delta). The closest major airports are BWI (a 3.5 hour drive), Pittsburgh (2.5 hours), and Newark (3.5 hours).

By Public Transit

You'll probably want to drive to get around State College, but if you have friends or family who will have a car, you can get to State College by train (Amtrak to Lewistown or Altoona) or bus (Megabus has a route between Chicago and New York that stops in State College, or you can take one of a couple Chinatown bus services from NYC)

By Canoe or Kayak

Spring Creek connects to the Susquehanna River, so guests coming from points south and east of State College could (with a few minor portages) paddle their way to the wedding. We suggest that guests choosing this means of transport leave a few extra days or weeks for travel, depending on paddling speed. You can search "water trails" here for maps and additional information.

By Foot

Pennsylvania is a state rich in public land, so guests wishing to make their way to State College on foot will find many options for senic trails. There are many trails covering the state, but the best option for most guests would be the Mid-State trail (wikipedia page) which passes the outskirts of State College, and forms part of the Great Eastern Trail, which connects both with the Finger Lakes Trail (a trail stretching to the southern end of Canandaigua Lake), and (indirectly) with the Appalachian Trail. As with those guests arriving by water, please be sure you leave enough time to make your journey!
